AND, OR, NOT, NAND, NOR, XOR. That's the whole foundation. Everything in a computer is built out of these.

Truth tables are just a way to exhaustively define what a gate does for every possible input combination. Boolean algebra gives you rules for simplifying combinations of gates. Karnaugh maps are a visual shortcut for the same simplification -- honestly more useful for small circuits than going through the algebra by hand.

Flip flops are where things get interesting. A flip flop stores one bit of state. Connect enough of them together with the right logic and you've got registers, counters, memory. The concept of "state that persists across clock cycles" is the bridge from combinational logic (stateless, output depends only on current input) to sequential logic (stateful, output depends on input AND history).

The reason this knowledge is worth having even if you're mostly a software person: when you're debugging weird hardware behavior or working with anything close to the metal, understanding how gates and flip flops work helps you reason about what's actually happening. Timing issues, metastability, race conditions -- these make a lot more sense once you understand the physical layer.

There's also something satisfying about seeing the connection between a NAND gate and a high-level abstraction like a memory address. It's all the same thing, just at different levels of the stack.
