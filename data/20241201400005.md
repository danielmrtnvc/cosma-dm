Not all metrics are equal. The difference between a vanity metric and a signal is whether it changes how you make decisions.

Daily active users sounds good until you ask: active how? Logging in counts as "active" in a lot of tracking setups. Logging in and completing a core workflow is different. The metric you pick is the behavior you're incentivizing your team to optimize for. Pick wrong and you optimize for the wrong thing.

The question I ask to evaluate any metric: "if this number went up, would I know the product is working better?" If the answer is yes, it might be a signal. If the answer is "maybe, but it could also mean something else," it's probably a vanity metric.

For Nodalli, the north star metric is reply rate on outbound sequences. Not emails sent, not opens, not even clicks. Replies. Because a reply means a real person engaged enough to type back, which is the behavior we're trying to enable and measure. Opens can be inflated by email clients. Clicks can be accidental. Replies are hard to fake.

Lagging vs leading indicators: lagging indicators (revenue, churn) tell you what already happened. Leading indicators (engagement patterns, specific usage behaviors) predict what's about to happen. You want both, but leading indicators are what you actually manage to.

The danger of over-indexing on any single metric: people will find ways to hit the number without achieving the underlying goal. Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.
