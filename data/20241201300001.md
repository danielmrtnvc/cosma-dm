At some point I started seeing everything as a potential automation. Not in a compulsive way, but as a genuine lens: "is this something I have to do manually every time, or is this something I could set up once and let run."

The more useful version of the question is "should I automate this." Not everything should be automated. Automating a process locks it in -- if the underlying thing changes, you have to update the automation too. Sometimes doing it manually is the right call because you're still figuring out what the process even is.

The concrete version of this at Nodalli: what was taking 25+ hours of manual work per week (research, writing, sending sequences) got compressed to under 5 hours by automating the repetitive parts. The automations didn't replace judgment -- they removed the low-judgment work so there was more time for the high-judgment work.

The things automation can't replace: figuring out what to automate, knowing when the automation is wrong, and anything that requires reading a situation in real-time. A good automation handles the obvious cases and escalates the edge cases.

This mindset connects to [[20241201200003|Building Before Validating]] in an interesting way: the right order is to do it manually first (to understand the process), then automate. Automating before you understand the process means you're encoding your current misunderstanding into a system that runs without you.
