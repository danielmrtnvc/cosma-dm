Fourier transforms, sampling, convolution, filtering. This course was the one where I thought "when will I ever use this" and then immediately used it.

The Fourier transform takes a signal in the time domain and shows you what frequencies it's made of. Every signal -- audio, radio, images -- can be decomposed into a sum of sine waves at different frequencies. This is the basis of basically all of signal processing.

Sampling is the process of converting a continuous signal (like sound in the air) into discrete values you can work with digitally. The Nyquist theorem says you need to sample at least twice the highest frequency you care about, or you get aliasing -- artifacts where higher frequencies "fold back" and look like lower frequencies. This is why audio is sampled at 44.1kHz (covering frequencies up to ~22kHz, which is above human hearing).

Convolution was the hardest thing in this course for me. The math is fine, the intuition takes a while. The way it finally made sense: convolution is how you apply a filter to a signal. Blur a photo? Convolution. Apply reverb to audio? Convolution. The filter (kernel) describes the operation, the signal is the input.

Where this connected directly to real work: the OCT project. Optical coherence tomography is basically interferometry, which is a signal processing problem. Understanding Fourier analysis was the difference between treating the signal processing code as a black box and actually understanding what it was doing. That was a meaningful moment.
