A good ticket is a gift to your future self and your team. A bad ticket is a tax -- everyone has to interrupt someone to ask a question that should have been answered in the ticket.

What needs to be in a ticket:

Context: why does this exist? What problem does it solve? What broke or what opportunity did we identify? Without this, engineers are solving for the letter of the requirement, not the spirit.

Acceptance criteria: what does "done" look like? Not just "the feature works" -- specifically, what behavior should a user be able to perform after this is shipped. Acceptance criteria also make it easier to write tests.

The "why now": where does this fit in the bigger picture? What becomes possible after this is done?

Gotchas: anything you know about that's likely to be surprising. Third-party API quirks, known edge cases, things that are technically in scope but should be handled carefully.

The tickets that caused the most confusion had one thing in common: they described what to build without explaining why. Engineers made reasonable assumptions about intent, built something technically correct, and it wasn't what was needed. Everyone did their job and the result was still wrong.

Getting better at this requires having been on both sides of it -- writing tickets and implementing them. Once you've been frustrated by your own bad tickets, you start writing better ones.
