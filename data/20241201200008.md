Everyone talks about product market fit and nobody can clearly define it. The definition that stuck with me: when you have it, growth starts pulling you instead of you pushing it. Users are showing up on their own, existing users are telling other people, the product is solving a real problem well enough that people want more of it.

Before PMF, you're pushing everything. Every user is hard-won. Every conversation has to start with explaining why this exists. After PMF, those things get easier. Not easy -- easier.

The hard part is you can convince yourself you have it when you don't. Early users can be enthusiastic without being genuinely sticky. People can like what you've built without it changing their behavior in a real way. The test isn't "do people like it" -- it's "would they be genuinely upset if it went away."

For Nodalli, the proxy metric we've been watching is reply rate on outbound sequences. Not open rate, not click rate -- reply rate. Because a reply means a real human typed out a response, which means they engaged with the content at a level that matters. That's the behavior we're trying to produce, so that's what we measure.

The danger of false PMF: you optimize for the wrong signal and run out of runway before finding out. Vanity metrics are comfortable. They feel like progress. They're not the same as retention and growth from genuine product value.

PMF isn't binary either. You might have it with one segment and not another. Finding which segment is a whole problem in itself.
