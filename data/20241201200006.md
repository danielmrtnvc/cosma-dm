Pitching is a learnable skill. I don't think I believed that initially -- it seemed like something some people were naturally good at and others weren't. That's not quite right.

The structure that works: problem, solution, why now, traction or evidence, team, ask. That's the skeleton. The job is to make it feel like a story rather than a slide deck being read aloud.

The most important part is the problem. If the audience doesn't believe the problem is real, everything else collapses. So you spend time on the problem first. Make them feel it before you introduce the solution.

Reading the room in front of investors is a skill. Some want data, some want narrative, some want to poke holes and see how you respond. The best pitches I've seen leave room for conversation instead of being airtight monologues.

Pitches that worked:
- DMZ $15K: the problem framing was sharp, we had real users, and we knew our numbers cold
- Samsung competition: we led with a demo instead of slides, which changed the energy in the room

What made them land in both cases: we believed in what we were saying and we weren't overselling. Investors can feel the difference between confidence and desperation.

The connection to [[20241201200010|Storytelling]] is real. Pitching is storytelling with a specific structure and a specific ask at the end. The same skills that make a good pitch make good product positioning, good hiring conversations, good everything.
