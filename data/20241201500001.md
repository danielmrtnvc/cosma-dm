The transition from thinking like an engineer to thinking like a PM isn't a clean switch. It's more like adding a second lens that you look through simultaneously. The engineers who become good PMs are the ones who kept both.

The engineer's lens is "how do we build this correctly." The PM's lens is "why are we building this and is it the right thing to build." Both matter. Pure PMs and pure engineers often talk past each other because they're optimizing for different things. Someone who can move between both modes is actually the useful one in the room.

Where the switch started for me: there was a moment, around the time I was managing engineers at uCast, where I realized I was spending more time thinking about what to build than how to build it. Not because I stopped caring about the how -- but because I was getting more value from thinking at the other level.

The technical credibility doesn't go away, it just changes form. I'm not the one writing the most code anymore, but being able to read the code, understand the tradeoffs, and have a real conversation about implementation -- that's what makes me useful in a technical conversation versus just someone who manages technical people.

The thing pure PMs miss: when you understand the code, you understand what's actually hard versus what just seems hard. You have a much better instinct for when an estimate is padded versus when there's real complexity. That calibration is a superpower.

The thing pure engineers sometimes miss: users don't care how it's built. They care whether it solves their problem. The architecture that's beautiful but doesn't serve a real need is a dead end.
