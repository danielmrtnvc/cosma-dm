The only way to know if something works is to ship it and see what happens. Everything before that is theory.

This sounds obvious but it's surprisingly hard to internalize. There's always a reason to wait: one more edge case to handle, one more round of feedback to gather, one more thing to polish. At some point you have to make a decision that "good enough to learn from" is a higher priority than "perfect."

The bias toward action has to be built into the team's culture, not just stated in a values document. It shows up in how you respond when something ships with a bug ("fix it fast and move on" vs "let's do a post-mortem process that takes three weeks"), how you frame the sprint goal ("ship this feature" vs "learn whether users want this feature"), and what you celebrate.

The difference between startup pace and enterprise pace is real. At a startup, you ship something, see what happens, adjust. The cycle is measured in days or weeks. At an enterprise, the cycle is measured in quarters or years, because the cost of being wrong is higher and the blast radius of a mistake is larger. Neither is wrong -- they're appropriate to different contexts.

What you lose at startup pace: depth. You're not always thinking carefully about long-term implications. What you gain: speed of learning. You find out faster whether your assumptions were right.

The cultural thing that enables fast shipping is psychological safety. People ship faster when they're not afraid of being blamed if something doesn't work. That's a leadership problem before it's a process problem.
