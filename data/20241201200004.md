MVP doesn't mean a buggy or incomplete version of your full product idea. That misunderstanding causes a lot of waste.

An MVP is the minimum thing needed to test your most critical assumption. The emphasis is on "test an assumption," not "ship something small." Those are different objectives.

If your critical assumption is "small restaurants want to manage reservations digitally," your MVP might not be software at all. It might be you manually managing reservations over text for a few restaurants to see if they stick with it and pay you. That's a valid MVP. Building a lightweight app to test the same thing is also valid, but the app isn't the point -- the learning is.

The question to ask for any feature or product: "what's the cheapest way to test whether this assumption is true?" Usually the answer is something simpler than what you're tempted to build.

This connects to [[20241119235908|Prioritization Frameworks]] because the same logic applies to what you build next. The highest-priority thing is usually whatever kills or confirms your biggest open question.

The failure mode: building an MVP that doesn't actually test anything critical, because you unconsciously avoided testing the scary thing. An MVP that tests a "nice to have" assumption instead of the existential one gives you false confidence.
