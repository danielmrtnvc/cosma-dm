Co-founder relationships are like marriages. You're spending more time with this person than almost anyone else in your life, you're making high-stakes decisions together under stress, and the natural conflict between different working styles gets amplified by everything that's going wrong on any given week.

The things that kill co-founder relationships aren't usually one big fight. It's the accumulation of small frictions that never get addressed. Different risk tolerances. Different expectations about work hours. Different ideas about what "done" looks like.

The most important thing to figure out early: who decides what. Not "we'll discuss and reach consensus" -- that works fine until there's real disagreement, and then it doesn't work at all. You need clear ownership of domains. Technical decisions belong to the technical co-founder. Product decisions belong to the product co-founder. When something spans domains, you need a tiebreaker rule.

The technical/non-technical divide is a specific tension. The technical person tends to underestimate the difficulty of sales, marketing, and relationship-building. The non-technical person tends to underestimate the difficulty of building reliable software. Both of these are real skills. Respecting each other's domain is the foundation.

What kills teams that aren't killed by the market: usually some combination of unclear ownership, unspoken resentment about contribution levels, and avoiding hard conversations until they're unavoidable. The startups that make it through tend to have founders who can disagree out loud, without it becoming personal.
