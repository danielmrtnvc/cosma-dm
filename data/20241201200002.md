Most early startups build solutions looking for a problem. It feels like building -- you're making progress, the code is coming together, the design looks good. But you haven't actually validated that anyone needs what you're making.

The difference between a problem you think exists and one people will pay to solve is enormous. And you can't tell which one you have by thinking harder about it. You have to go talk to people.

Talking to potential users is harder than it sounds. The natural instinct is to explain your idea and ask if they'd use it. That's backwards. You're leading the witness. People are polite; they'll tell you your idea is interesting even when they wouldn't use it in a million years.

The technique that works: ask about their actual behavior, not their hypothetical behavior. "Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem]" is better than "would you pay for a tool that solved [problem]." Behavior is evidence. Intentions are not.

With uCast, we built a lot before we had good evidence that campus event organizers had the problem we thought they had. Some of them did. Some of them had a different problem. Some of them weren't actually frustrated enough to change anything. We found this out later than we should have.

With Nodalli, the feedback loop was faster because we were closer to the problem space. But even then, the "right" problem took months of iteration to find.

Finding the real problem is probably the hardest thing in a startup. It's also the most important.
