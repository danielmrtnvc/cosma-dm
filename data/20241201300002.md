Prompt engineering is more like writing good specs than programming. You're not telling the model how to do something, you're telling it what you want and giving it enough context to figure out the how.

The mental model I find most useful: the model is a very smart, very literal intern who has read an enormous amount but has no context about your specific situation. It will do exactly what you ask, not what you meant. This means precision matters.

Techniques that actually work:

Role assignment: "You are a senior engineer reviewing code for security issues" changes the output more than you'd expect. The model uses the role as context for what lens to apply.

Examples in the prompt (few-shot): show the model a few examples of the format or style you want, and it'll match it much better than if you describe it in words. Describing what you want is harder than showing it.

Chain of thought: asking the model to "think step by step" or "explain your reasoning" before giving a final answer improves accuracy on reasoning tasks. It's not magic -- it gives the model room to work through the problem before committing to an answer.

What doesn't work: being vague and hoping. "Make it better" is a terrible prompt. "Improve the clarity of this explanation for a non-technical audience without changing the key points" is much better.

The gap most people don't talk about: a prompt that works in the playground doesn't always work reliably at scale. Edge cases, unusual inputs, and real-world messiness break prompts that seemed solid in testing. See [[20241201300003|LLMs in Production]] for more on that.
