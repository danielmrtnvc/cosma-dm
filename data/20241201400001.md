The hardest part of user research is not leading the witness. It's almost impossible to ask neutral questions once you have a hypothesis about what the answer should be.

The classic mistake: "Would you find it helpful if you could see all your tasks in one place?" Nobody is going to say no to that. It's phrased as a solution and solutions sound good in the abstract. The answer tells you nothing.

Better: "Tell me about the last time you felt like you were losing track of something important at work. What happened?" Now you're getting behavior, not opinions about a hypothetical. Behavior is the signal.

The other thing that trips people up: assuming what someone says they want is what they actually need. Users are great at describing their problem and not great at describing the solution. If someone says "I need a better dashboard," don't build a better dashboard -- ask what they'd use the dashboard to do, and then ask what's frustrating about doing that now.

From Nodalli user interviews: we kept hearing "we need better templates." But when we dug in, the real problem was that people didn't know what to write in the messages, not that the template format was wrong. Better templates would have been the wrong solution to the real problem.

The discipline is asking one more "why" than feels comfortable. The answer to the first "why" is usually the symptom. You want the cause.
