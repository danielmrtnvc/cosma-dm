Fundraising is a sales process. The product you're selling is equity in your company. The buyer is an investor with a specific thesis about what kinds of companies are worth betting on.

With uCast we raised around $200K, mostly through grants and accelerator programs in Canada. That process taught me a lot about what investors and grant organizations actually look for, which is different from what they say they look for.

VCs want to see: large market, founder-market fit, some evidence of traction or exceptional insight, and a reason why you'll win. They're pattern-matching to their mental model of what a successful company in this space looks like. Fitting that pattern matters as much as the actual quality of the idea.

The grant game is different. Grants are non-dilutive (you don't give up equity) but come with more process, slower timelines, and sometimes weird constraints on how the money can be used. The organizations running them are often optimizing for things other than pure ROI -- regional economic development, sector support, that kind of thing.

Accelerators are somewhere in between. The money is usually small (15K-100K range) but the network and the forced structure of the program can be worth more than the cash, depending on which accelerator and what stage you're at.

One thing that took me a while to understand: money doesn't validate your idea. It validates your ability to fundraise, which correlates with but isn't the same thing as having a good idea. Some great ideas can't raise, and some bad ideas do. The market is the real test.
