The art of giving people enough information to feel confident without overwhelming them. Every stakeholder has a different context and a different set of things they need to know. The communication style that works for an engineer doesn't work for an investor and doesn't work for a customer.

Managing up (to founders or executives): they want to know what changed, what's next, and what's at risk. Not the how -- the what and the so-what. Keep it short. Flag problems before they become surprises.

Managing across (to other teams or functions): depends on the relationship, but generally: lead with what you need, provide the minimum context required to evaluate that, and make it easy to say yes or no. People are busy.

Managing down (to your team): context matters more here. People do better work when they understand why. "Build this feature because users asked for it" is okay. "Build this feature because it removes the biggest friction in the onboarding flow, which we know is where we're losing 40% of users" is better.

The one rule that's never wrong: no surprises. If something is going wrong, tell people before it's a crisis. A stakeholder who finds out about a problem from you has a chance to help. A stakeholder who finds out on their own is now a problem.

The update format that works: what changed since last time, what's happening next, what's at risk or blocked. Three sections. Stays short. Gives people what they need to stay oriented without requiring them to ask follow-up questions.
