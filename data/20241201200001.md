A startup isn't just a small company. It's a company designed to find a scalable business model, usually under conditions of extreme uncertainty. That distinction matters because it changes how you should make decisions.

Most people who say they're "starting a company" are actually building a project. A project has a defined scope. A startup has a hypothesis. The whole point is to test the hypothesis and update based on what you find.

The thing nobody tells you at the beginning: the emotional experience of a startup is mostly just confusion and doubt punctuated by occasional moments of clarity. The chaos is the job. You're not doing it wrong.

What I've learned from going through it with uCast and then Nodalli:
- The version of the idea you start with is almost never the version that eventually works
- The quality of the team is more predictive than the quality of the initial idea
- Speed matters more than most people think, but rushing is different from speed
- Most startup advice is pattern-matched from a small number of successful companies and generalizes badly

It's a weird thing to pursue. It's also one of the most interesting ways to spend your time.
