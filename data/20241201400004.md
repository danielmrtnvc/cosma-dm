The thing that makes leading engineers work is technical credibility. You don't have to be the best engineer on the team, but you need to be able to have a real conversation about technical tradeoffs. If engineers sense that you're bullshitting them about technical complexity, they'll stop telling you things honestly.

The difference between managing and leading: managing is making sure work gets done. Leading is making sure people understand why the work matters and feel ownership over how to do it. The second one scales better and produces better outcomes.

How to give feedback on code without being condescending: ask questions rather than making declarations. "I noticed this function is doing several things -- is there a reason it's structured this way?" lands differently than "this should be broken up." You might not know what you don't know. Asking first finds out.

When to push back on estimates vs trust the team: if an estimate seems high, ask what's driving it. Often there's a real technical reason you weren't aware of. Sometimes there's unnecessary complexity that can be removed. The conversation is more useful than either blindly accepting or arbitrarily cutting.

At Nodalli and uCast, the dynamics that worked were when technical and non-technical decisions were clearly separated. Engineers owned the how. PMs owned the what and why. The overlap is where the tension lives, and being explicit about it helps.

The thing I'd tell myself earlier: "move fast" doesn't mean "skip the conversation." Moving fast is about decision quality and removing blockers, not skipping the alignment that prevents rework.
