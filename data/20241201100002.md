Sorting, searching, recursion, dynamic programming. These are the big categories. Each one clicked at a different time for me.

Sorting felt mechanical at first -- here's bubble sort, here's merge sort, here's quicksort. The understanding came later when I realized why it matters to pick the right one depending on whether your data is mostly sorted, what your memory constraints are, whether you need stability.

Recursion took a while. The mental block is that you have to trust the recursive call works before you've seen it work. Once I stopped trying to trace the full call stack in my head and just focused on "what does this function need to return, and can I express that in terms of a smaller version of itself" -- that's when it unlocked.

Dynamic programming is basically recursion plus memoization, but the framing matters. Thinking about it as "cache the subproblems you've already solved" is more intuitive than the textbook bottom-up table approach. Though the table approach is usually faster in practice.

The insight that changed everything: most algorithm problems, if you squint, are some form of graph traversal. DFS, BFS, Dijkstra's, they're all the same idea at different levels of generality. Once you see that pattern you stop treating each problem type as totally separate.
