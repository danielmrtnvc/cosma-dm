The ones that actually matter: arrays, linked lists, hash maps, trees, graphs. That's basically it. Everything else is a variant or a specialization of one of these.

The mental model I find useful is "what problem does this structure solve." Arrays are for when you know the index. Hash maps are for when you know the key. Trees are for when things are hierarchically related or you need sorted access. Graphs are for when relationships go in multiple directions.

In real projects I reach for arrays and hash maps constantly. Linked lists almost never, unless I'm implementing something lower-level. Trees show up more than you'd think once you start working with file systems or any kind of hierarchical data.

Interview culture has given data structures a weird reputation -- like it's all about memorizing the worst-case complexity of a red-black tree rotation. But the actual skill is recognizing which structure fits the problem in front of you. That's pattern matching, not memorization.

The thing that clicked for me was realizing most "hard" algorithm problems are just graph traversal with extra steps. Once you see that, the whole field starts to feel more coherent.
