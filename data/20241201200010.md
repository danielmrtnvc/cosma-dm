The most underrated startup skill is the ability to explain clearly what you're doing and why it matters. Not just for investors -- for recruiting, for users, for yourself when you're in the middle of something hard and you need to remember why you started.

A lot of founders can't clearly explain their own product after working on it for a year. They've gotten too deep into their own heads. The features blur together, the positioning is vague, the "why it matters" gets buried under the "how it works."

Good storytelling forces clarity of thought. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it simply. Complexity in the explanation usually reflects complexity in the thinking.

Building Nodalli's positioning was one of the more clarifying exercises I've done. We had to compress what the product actually does into language that a potential customer can immediately understand -- not a paragraph of context, not a list of features. The short, true version. That process of compression forced us to decide what the product actually was.

The structure I keep coming back to: here's a problem that's real, here's why the existing solutions don't solve it, here's what we've built to solve it differently. That's it. Three sentences. If you can make all three land, the rest of the conversation goes better.

This connects to [[20241120200355|Writing Process]] because writing is thinking. The act of putting words on a page forces you to commit to a perspective in a way that thinking in your head doesn't. The rough draft is how you find out what you actually think.
