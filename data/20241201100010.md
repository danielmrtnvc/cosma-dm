Relational vs NoSQL, SQL, indexing, transactions. The database decisions you make early in a project will haunt you later. This is not a metaphor.

SQL is worth knowing well. The mental model: tables are sets, queries are set operations. Joins, aggregations, subqueries -- once the set-theory framing clicks, the syntax makes sense. The weird stuff (GROUP BY, window functions) is just set operations with extra constraints.

Indexing is the thing everyone understands conceptually but forgets to do in practice. Without an index, a query scans every row. With an index on the right column, the DB can jump directly to the relevant rows. Adding an index to the right column on a table with a million rows can turn a 30-second query into a millisecond one. The tradeoff: indexes slow down writes, because the index has to be updated too. Choose what you index based on your read/write pattern.

Transactions (ACID) give you atomicity, consistency, isolation, durability. The practical version: a transaction either fully succeeds or fully fails, and other transactions can't see its partial state. This is what prevents your database from getting into a weird half-updated state when something goes wrong.

The denormalization question: you normalize to avoid duplication and maintain consistency, you denormalize to make reads faster. Most applications should start normalized and denormalize only when there's a specific, measured performance problem. Premature denormalization is a trap.

ORMs are useful but they hide things you should understand. Knowing what SQL your ORM is generating is the difference between a fast app and an app that accidentally loads 10,000 rows to display 10.
