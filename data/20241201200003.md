This is one of the most common and most painful startup mistakes. You have an idea, it feels obvious and urgent, so you start building. Weeks or months later you have a product and you go to show it to users and find out the assumptions it was built on were wrong.

The trap is that building feels productive. You're making decisions, writing code, creating things. The uncomfortable alternative -- going out and talking to people before you have anything to show -- feels like stalling. But it's usually the right move.

The rationalization I've heard (and made myself): "I need to build something before I can get real feedback." Sometimes true. Mostly an excuse to avoid the discomfort of not knowing if your idea is good.

What I'd do differently: treat the first few months as pure customer discovery, not product development. The goal is to build a model of the problem space that's grounded in real conversations, not assumptions. Then build the minimum thing needed to test the highest-risk assumption.

The version of this that happened with an early project: spent two months on features that users didn't care about and hadn't asked for, because we thought we knew what they needed. We didn't. We found out eventually, but we'd burned runway and time on the wrong things.

The cost of a wrong assumption is much lower before you've built around it.
