Two-week sprints work because the rhythm matters as much as the process. You're creating regular forcing functions: a moment every two weeks where the team commits to something, ships it, and reflects on what happened.

The goal of sprint planning isn't to fill the sprint with tasks. It's to define what success looks like for the sprint and then figure out what work gets you there. "The sprint is done when X" is more useful than a list of tickets.

The estimation problem is real and basically unsolvable. Engineers are systematically optimistic. Not because they're bad at their jobs -- because software estimation is genuinely hard and the unknown unknowns are unknowable in advance. The practical fix: don't use time estimates, use story points or t-shirt sizes, and track your team's actual velocity over a few sprints. Then plan based on history, not hope.

The difference between a sprint goal and a sprint task list: the goal is what you're trying to learn or deliver. The task list is how you get there. If something comes up mid-sprint that would help you achieve the goal faster, you should be able to add it. If a task in the plan turns out not to serve the goal, you should be able to drop it.

The overhead question: sprint planning only feels like overhead if the meetings are badly run. A good planning session takes an hour, produces a clear shared understanding of what the team is doing and why, and prevents the much more expensive cost of building the wrong thing.
