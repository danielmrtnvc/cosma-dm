The most leveraged thing you do as a lead is get the team right. One exceptional person is worth several good ones, not because of their individual output but because they raise the level of everyone around them. One consistently wrong fit is expensive in ways that are hard to measure but very real.

What to actually look for in engineers, beyond technical skill: curiosity (do they learn on their own, do they care about the craft) and communication (can they explain what they're doing and why, can they raise a problem before it's a crisis). Technical skill is table stakes. These other things are what predict whether someone will thrive in a small team under ambiguous conditions.

The interview question that tells me the most: "tell me about something technical you taught yourself recently." Motivated learners self-study. People going through the motions don't. The answer also tells you about their taste -- what they find interesting when they have free choice.

The mistakes made early in hiring at uCast: optimizing for technical skill signals (good at algorithms, impressive background) over indicators of how someone works and communicates. Ended up with technically capable people who were hard to coordinate with. That's an expensive lesson to learn.

How to interview without trick questions: give candidates real problems similar to ones you actually face, see how they think through them, and look for people who ask clarifying questions before jumping to solutions. The process of how they engage with a problem is more informative than whether they get to the right answer.

The timing thing: hire when you have a specific need and the work is validated, not speculatively for capacity you might need. Early hiring mistakes at startups are very hard to undo.
