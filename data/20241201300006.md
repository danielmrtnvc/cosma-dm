The shift that AI tools actually caused in day-to-day work isn't that they replaced what I was doing -- it's that they changed what "the work" is.

Before tools like Cursor and Claude, most of the time writing code was spent on the typing and syntax. Looking things up, remembering APIs, fixing the obvious structural errors. That's still necessary but it's much faster now.

What's left is the part that was always the actual job: figuring out what to build, why it should work the way you're proposing, what the edge cases are, how it fits into the larger system. The thinking that happens before and after the typing. That part hasn't been automated -- it's become more prominent because the surrounding work is faster.

What AI is genuinely good at: first drafts, boilerplate, translating a clear intention into working code, explaining unfamiliar code, catching obvious errors. What it's not good at: understanding subtle constraints you haven't told it about, knowing which of several valid approaches is right for your specific situation, anything requiring judgment about what matters.

The skill shift is real. Less time typing, more time thinking about what to type. More time reviewing output critically rather than generating it from scratch. That's a different skill profile than pure coding. Getting good at directing AI tools is its own thing.

The connection to [[20241121123135|AI and Humans: Will We Achieve Symbiosis?]] is live: this is what human-AI collaboration looks like in practice. Not the sci-fi version -- just a much smarter autocomplete that changes what you spend your mental energy on.
