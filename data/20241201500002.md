Everything is a system. The mental model that helps you understand how a CPU works also helps you understand how a company works. Once you have the framework, it applies everywhere.

The basic elements: inputs, outputs, feedback loops, and failure modes. A CPU takes instructions as input, produces computed results as output, uses internal state that affects future behavior, and has failure modes (segfaults, race conditions) when assumptions are violated. A company takes capital and work as inputs, produces product and revenue as outputs, uses customer feedback to update direction, and has failure modes (runway, team breakdown, market shift) when assumptions are violated.

The feedback loop part is the most important and the most neglected. Systems without feedback loops drift. A company that doesn't measure whether its output is creating value is flying blind. A CPU without error correction produces garbage silently. The feedback mechanism is what keeps a system honest.

Failure modes are worth thinking about explicitly. For software: what breaks when the input is unexpected? For a startup: what kills the company if the main assumption is wrong? For an automation: what happens when the third-party API goes down? Thinking through failure modes isn't pessimism -- it's the thing that makes systems resilient rather than brittle.

Computer engineering gave me this framework. The habit of thinking in terms of abstractions, interfaces, and failure modes came from building and debugging actual systems. It's one of those things where the domain expertise transfers beyond the original domain in ways you don't expect.
